Indentifying Argument Structures

Previous Next

Argumentation is an essential skill in scientific domains including physics, engineering, and computer science, where students must articulate and justify testable hypotheses through argumentative reasoning. As a consequence, automated essay grading systems have become particularly useful tools. While argument structures are essential for evaluating the quality of an argument, the goal of this project is to automatically extract the argumentative structures from natural language corpora.

To classify the argument components and relationships, we propose to use bidirectional DAG-structured RNN model to incorporate discourse features into deep neural network, where the discourse features include discourse markers, syntactic features of POS tagging and Named Entity, and lexical features of N-grams. We expect that the proposed model can outform the state of art results of applying SVM and maximum entropy model on the manually extracted discourse features. We will also work on building an End-to-End deep learning model to automatically extract the argument structures from texts using RNN and Convolutional Neural Networks.

Argument Diagram Analysis

Previous Next

Augmented Graph Grammars are a robust rule formalism for graphs. Research has shown that hand-authored graph grammars can be used to automatically grade argument diagrams. Argument diagrams are graphical representations that reify key features of arguments such as hypothesis statements, claims, and citations as nodes and the supporting, opposing, and clarification relationships between them as arcs.

In this work, we applied Evolutionary Computation (EC) to automatically induce two types of empirically-valid graph grammars for student-produced argument diagrams. The positive-correlated graph rules are good structural features, and the negative-correlated graph rules indicate the structural flaws in students’ work. We showed that the induced graph rules outperform the expert rules and they outperform all of the rules induced by two general purpose graph grammar induction algorithms, Subdue and gSpan.

The significance of this project is that the induced graph rules can be used for automatic grading and providing the basis for hints.

Digital Human Model for Ergonomic Evaluation

Previous Next

Laparoscopic techniques provide significant advantages to patients, such as shorter hospital stays, faster recovery times, and minimal scarring. However, poor ergonomics in laparoscopic surgery and failure to understand the ergonomics of laparoscopic surgery increase the discomfort and fatigue of the surgeon. According to a questionnaire by SAGES (Society of American Gastrointestinal Endoscopic Surgeons), a large number of laparoscopic surgeons suffer frequent pain of the necks, and upper extremities pain. One of the critical factors that causes these pains is the poorly adapted operation table height.

The aim of this study is to investigate new technical solutions from ergonomic aspect to reduce the stress and strain on the surgeon during laparoscopic procedures. This was attempted by digital human modeling and its posture prediction when it is integrated into the operating room (OR) environment.

Social Network Analysis in Blended Courses

The large-scale online management systems (e.g. Moodle), online web forums (e.g. Piazza), and online homework systems (e.g. WebAssign) have been widely used in the blended courses recently. Instructors can use these systems to deliver class content and materials. Students can communicate with the classmates, share the course materials, and discuss the course questions via the online forums. With the increased use of the online systems, a large amount of students' interaction data has been collected. This data can be used to analyze students' learning behaviors and predict students' learning outcomes.

In this work, we collected students' interaction data in three different blended courses. We represented the data as directed graphs and investigated the correlation between the social graph properties and students' final grades. Our results showed that in all these classes, students who asked more answers and received more feedbacks on the forum tend to obtain higher grades. The significance of this work is that we can use the results to encourage students to participate more in forums to learn the class materials better; we can also build a predictive model based on the social metrics to show us low performing students early in the semester.

Neural Event Detection

A neural event detection system has a widely application in gaming devices where the system can facilitate human-computer interaction and can improve user experience. In this project, I worked in a team of two to develop a deep learning model for the neural event (liking or disliking) detection. The experiments were conducted based on DEAP dataset, which is a multimodal dataset for the analysis of human affective states.

We implemented several different feature extraction methods that include: channel selection, EEG words learning, and feature representation. We utilized the Deep Belief Network to classify the neural event of liking and disliking. The accuracy and AUC score of our model were improved 26% and 29% respectively over the baseline model of SVM and logistic regression.

Image Classification

Image classification has been one of the major research areas in both computer vision community and machine learning community for a long time. In this project, I worked in a team of two to explore deep learning algorithms to classify image dataset of CIFAR-10.

We utilized two deep learning models of Stacked Denoising Autoencoder (SDAE) and Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). We showed that CNN model outperforms the SDAE model and the baseline model of SVM.
